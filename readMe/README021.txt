## What Is a Stock?

A stock, also called a share or equity, represents **ownership in a company**. Purchasing a stock gives the investor a claim on the company’s **assets and future earnings**.

- **Institutional perspective:** Stocks are **vehicles for deploying capital efficiently** and strategically allocating risk.
- **Retail perspective:** Stocks are often viewed as “things to buy,” but professional traders analyze **mechanics, risk, and structure** before entering positions.

> In essence, a stock is a **tradable unit of business risk and future expectation**, not just a price to follow.

---

## Historical & Conceptual Context: Methodology Creators

### Richard D. Wyckoff (1873–1934)
- Pioneer in market behavior analysis
- Developed the concept of **market phases** (accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown)
- Studied **institutional behavior** and its impact on price movement

### Ralph Nelson Elliott (1871–1948)
- Developed **Elliott Wave Theory**
- Observed that **markets move in repetitive patterns reflecting collective human psychology**
- Emphasized **impulse and corrective waves**, forecasting trends and corrections

### Leonardo Fibonacci (1170–1250)
- Mathematician known for the **Fibonacci sequence**
- Traders use **Fibonacci ratios** (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%) to identify **probable support, resistance, and retracement zones**
- Provides a **probabilistic lens** to anticipate market reactions

> These methodologies are **emphasis frameworks**, not dogma: they explain **why price behaves a certain way** in addition to what is happening.

---

## Why Companies Issue Stock

Companies issue stock to:

1. **Raise growth capital** without debt
2. **Transfer operational risk** to public investors
3. **Provide liquidity** for founders and early investors

> Wyckoff lens: Stock issuance creates opportunities for **institutional accumulation**  
> Elliott lens: Sentiment drives the **formation of waves**  
> Fibonacci lens: Retracements indicate **likely reaction zones** for both institutions and skilled traders

---

## How Stock Prices Are Determined

Stock prices are the result of **supply and demand dynamics**, influenced by:

1. **Order flow:** Buyers and sellers submit market, limit, and stop orders  
2. **Liquidity dynamics:** Large participants move markets more than retail  
3. **Perceived value:** News, earnings, and macro events shift expectations

> Wyckoff: Price moves in **phases**  
> Elliott: Price forms **waves of crowd psychology**  
> Fibonacci: Price often **respects proportional retracement levels**

---

## Real-World Example

A biotech stock with limited revenue may trade higher than a profitable manufacturer because:

- **Wyckoff:** Institutions accumulate quietly before price markup  
- **Elliott:** Impulsive waves are followed by corrective waves reflecting profit-taking and hesitation  
- **Fibonacci:** Retracement levels often act as **entry points** or **reaction zones**

> Understanding mechanics, psychology, and proportional behavior is key to **professional trading decisions**.

---

## Core Takeaway

> A stock is **more than an asset; it is a unit of risk, probability, and market psychology**.  
> Successful traders combine **mechanics, structure, and mental models** to evaluate positions and manage risk effectively.

---

## Glossary

- **Stock / Share / Equity:** Ownership interest in a company  
- **Accumulation:** Institutional buying phase (Wyckoff)  
- **Distribution:** Institutional selling phase (Wyckoff)  
- **Impulse Wave:** Directional movement (Elliott Wave)  
- **Corrective Wave:** Pullback or consolidation (Elliott Wave)  
- **Fibonacci Retracement:** Likely support/resistance based on Fibonacci ratios  
- **Liquidity:** Ease of buying or selling without large price impact  
- **Volatility:** Degree of price fluctuation
